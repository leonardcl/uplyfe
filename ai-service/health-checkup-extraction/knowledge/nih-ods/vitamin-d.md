# Vitamin D — NIH Office of Dietary Supplements

Source: NIH Office of Dietary Supplements. Vitamin D Fact Sheet for Health Professionals. https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminD-HealthProfessional/
NIH ODS fact sheets are public-domain US government work. This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval.

## What 25-OH vitamin D measures

Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] is the best indicator of overall vitamin D status because it reflects vitamin D from sun exposure and from food and supplements, and it has a long half-life (~15 days).

## Reference categories

NIH ODS uses these bands (ng/mL; multiply by 2.5 to get nmol/L):

- Below 12 — deficiency. Risk of rickets in infants and children, osteomalacia in adults.
- 12 to <20 — generally inadequate for bone and overall health in many adults.
- 20–50 — generally considered adequate.
- Above 50 — possibly high; levels above 100 can have adverse effects (hypercalcemia).

These cutoffs are population-level guidance; some clinical groups (Endocrine Society) use a higher "sufficiency" threshold of 30 ng/mL.

## Who is most likely to be low

- People with little sun exposure (high latitudes, indoor lifestyle, sunscreen use, covering clothing).
- Older adults (skin synthesizes less vitamin D).
- People with darker skin (melanin reduces UVB-driven synthesis).
- Obesity (vitamin D distributes into fat).
- Malabsorption (celiac, IBD, bariatric surgery, cystic fibrosis).
- Chronic kidney disease and severe liver disease (impaired activation).
- Exclusively breastfed infants without supplementation.

## Recommended daily intake (RDA)

- Children and adults 1–70 years: 600 IU/day (15 mcg).
- Adults >70 years: 800 IU/day (20 mcg).
- Upper limit (UL) for adults: 4000 IU/day (100 mcg) from supplements.

The RDA assumes minimal sun exposure.

## Repletion strategies

- Sun exposure (cautiously — UVB causes skin cancer at higher doses).
- Food sources: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), fortified milk and orange juice, egg yolks, fortified cereals, mushrooms exposed to UV light.
- Supplements: D3 (cholecalciferol) is generally preferred over D2 (ergocalciferol) for raising and maintaining levels.

Repletion regimens for documented deficiency are individualized with a clinician (commonly higher-dose loading regimens followed by maintenance).

## Practical context

Single low values are common and should be interpreted alongside calcium, PTH, and overall bone health. Avoid supranutritional dosing without clinician input — toxicity is real at sustained high intakes.
