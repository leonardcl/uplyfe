# Vitamin B12 — NIH Office of Dietary Supplements

Source: NIH Office of Dietary Supplements. Vitamin B12 Fact Sheet for Health Professionals. https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/VitaminB12-HealthProfessional/
NIH ODS fact sheets are public-domain US government work. This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval.

## What B12 does

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is required for red blood cell formation, DNA synthesis, and nervous system function. Deficiency causes macrocytic anemia and can cause neurologic symptoms — numbness, tingling, balance problems, cognitive changes — that can become irreversible if prolonged.

## Reference categories

Serum B12 (pg/mL; multiply by 0.738 for pmol/L):

- Below 200 — deficiency range.
- 200–300 — borderline; consider methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine to confirm functional deficiency.
- 300 or above — generally adequate.

Serum B12 alone has limitations; MMA and homocysteine are more sensitive markers of true tissue deficiency, particularly in the borderline range.

## Who is at higher risk

- Vegans and strict vegetarians (B12 occurs naturally only in animal-source foods and fortified products).
- Older adults — atrophic gastritis reduces B12 absorption.
- People on long-term proton pump inhibitors or H2 blockers.
- People on metformin long-term.
- People with pernicious anemia (autoimmune intrinsic factor deficiency).
- Prior gastric or ileal surgery.
- Inflammatory bowel disease, celiac disease.

## Recommended intake (adults)

- 2.4 mcg/day. Pregnancy 2.6 mcg/day, lactation 2.8 mcg/day.

There is no established upper limit because B12 is water-soluble and excess is excreted; toxicity is rare.

## Sources

Animal foods (meat, fish, dairy, eggs); fortified cereals; nutritional yeast (fortified). Plant foods generally do not contain B12 unless fortified.

## Practical context

Hemoglobin and MCV give clues — macrocytic anemia (MCV >100) with low B12 is the classic combination, but neurologic symptoms can occur with normal-appearing CBC. Borderline B12 with macrocytosis or neurologic symptoms is typically followed up with MMA and homocysteine, and treated with oral or intramuscular B12 depending on cause.
