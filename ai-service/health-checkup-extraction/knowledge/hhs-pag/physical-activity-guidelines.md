# HHS Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2nd Edition (2018)

Source: U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, 2nd Edition. 2018.
US government work; public domain. This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval.

## Headline: move more, sit less

The 2nd-edition guidelines emphasize that *any* physical activity counts toward weekly targets — there is no longer a 10-minute minimum bout requirement. Even brief activity replaces sedentary time and contributes to health.

## Adults (ages 18–64)

- Move more, sit less. Some physical activity is better than none.
- For substantial benefit: 150–300 minutes/week moderate-intensity OR 75–150 minutes/week vigorous-intensity aerobic, OR an equivalent combination.
- For greater benefit: more than 300 minutes/week moderate-intensity (or equivalent).
- Muscle-strengthening activities of moderate or greater intensity, involving all major muscle groups, on ≥2 days/week.

## Older adults (65+)

Same as adults, plus:

- Multicomponent physical activity that includes balance training and aerobic and muscle-strengthening activities.
- Be as physically active as abilities and conditions allow.
- People with chronic conditions: stay active, but understand whether and how chronic conditions affect ability to do activity safely.

## Pregnant and postpartum

- Aim for ≥150 minutes/week moderate aerobic activity during pregnancy and the postpartum period.
- Pregnancy is generally a safe time to be active. People who were highly active before pregnancy can usually continue, with adjustments for comfort and safety.
- Avoid activities with high fall risk, contact sports, hot environments, and prolonged supine positions in late pregnancy. Discuss with an obstetric clinician.

## Children and adolescents (6–17)

- ≥60 minutes/day of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity, mostly aerobic.
- Vigorous-intensity physical activity, muscle-strengthening, and bone-strengthening activities on ≥3 days/week.

## Behavioral key — replace sedentary time

The strongest predictor of cardiometabolic benefit at the population level is reducing sedentary time, not just hitting the weekly minutes target. Walking breaks, standing meetings, parking farther away, taking stairs — these accumulate.

## Cardiometabolic-specific takeaways

- For type 2 diabetes / prediabetes: combined aerobic plus resistance training has stronger glycemic effects than either alone.
- For hypertension: aerobic exercise lowers systolic BP by ~5 mmHg on average across studies.
- For weight management: 200–300 minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity is more effective than the 150-minute minimum.

## Safety screening

People starting a vigorous program who have established cardiovascular disease, suspected ischemia, severe orthopedic limitations, very poorly controlled hypertension, or recent significant lab abnormalities should discuss the plan with a clinician first. Stop and seek care for chest pain, severe shortness of breath, syncope, or new neurologic symptoms during exercise.
