# AHA — healthy eating pattern for cardiovascular health

Source: American Heart Association. 2021 Dietary Guidance to Improve Cardiovascular Health. Circulation.
This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval, not a verbatim reproduction.

## Ten features of a heart-healthy pattern

1. Adjust energy intake and expenditure to achieve and maintain a healthy weight.
2. Eat plenty and a variety of fruits and vegetables — choose whole forms over juice when possible.
3. Choose whole grain foods and products over refined grains.
4. Choose healthy sources of protein, mostly plant proteins, fish/seafood, low-fat or fat-free dairy, and lean cuts/forms of meat (and avoid processed meats).
5. Use liquid plant oils rather than tropical oils (coconut, palm, palm kernel), animal fats (e.g., butter, lard), and partially hydrogenated fats.
6. Choose minimally processed foods over ultra-processed foods.
7. Minimize beverages and foods with added sugars.
8. Choose and prepare foods with little or no salt.
9. If you do not consume alcohol, do not start; if you choose to drink alcohol, limit intake.
10. Apply this guidance regardless of where food is prepared or consumed.

## Saturated fat target

Limit saturated fat to <6% of total calories. Replace with unsaturated fats from plant oils, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish. This single change has the strongest LDL-lowering effect among dietary modifications.

## Soluble fiber

Aim for 5–10 g/day of soluble fiber (oats, beans, lentils, fruit). This dose reduces LDL cholesterol on average by ~5%. Plant sterols (~2 g/day, often via fortified spreads or supplements) can further lower LDL.

## Triglyceride-specific notes

When triglycerides are elevated:

- Reduce added sugars, especially fructose-containing sweeteners and sweetened beverages.
- Limit refined starches.
- Reduce alcohol intake — even moderate intake substantially raises triglycerides in susceptible people.
- Increase omega-3 intake (fatty fish 2 servings/week, or as advised).

## Blood-pressure-specific notes

DASH-style eating, sodium reduction toward 1500–2300 mg/day, and increased potassium-rich foods (unless kidney disease or potassium-elevating medications make this inadvisable) lower blood pressure.

## Practical context

Single-meal changes do not move lab values; *patterns over weeks to months* do. The strongest evidence is for the Mediterranean-style and DASH patterns; both are compatible with this AHA framing and adaptable across cultures and budgets.
