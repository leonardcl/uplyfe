# Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/hemoglobin-a1c-hba1c-test/

The HbA1c test measures the percentage of hemoglobin in red blood cells that is glycated (has glucose bound to it). Because red cells live about three months, HbA1c reflects average blood glucose over the prior 8–12 weeks. It does not require fasting and is not affected by recent meals.

## Reference categories

For non-pregnant adults:

- Normal: below 5.7%.
- Prediabetes: 5.7%–6.4%.
- Diabetes range: 6.5% or higher (confirmation usually required).

For people who already have a diabetes diagnosis, an HbA1c target around 7% is common, but goals are individualized — a clinician adjusts based on age, comorbidities, and risk of hypoglycemia.

## Conditions that distort HbA1c

HbA1c can be falsely high or low when red-cell turnover or hemoglobin structure is unusual:

- Iron-deficiency anemia and recent blood loss can raise HbA1c.
- Hemolytic anemia, recent transfusion, and certain hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell trait, thalassemia, HbE) can lower or otherwise distort it.
- Chronic kidney disease and pregnancy alter the relationship between HbA1c and average glucose.

When HbA1c and fasting glucose disagree, the disagreement is itself useful — it suggests one of these confounders, recent acute hyperglycemia, or laboratory variation.

## Practical context

A single HbA1c in the diabetes range is not a clinical diagnosis. Repeat testing or correlation with another assay (fasting glucose or OGTT) is standard. Markedly elevated values (≥10%) warrant prompt clinician follow-up; values consistently above 9% on therapy may indicate a need for treatment intensification.

Lifestyle factors that move HbA1c down: weight loss in those with overweight/obesity, regular aerobic and resistance exercise, reduced added sugar and refined-carbohydrate intake, and adequate sleep.
