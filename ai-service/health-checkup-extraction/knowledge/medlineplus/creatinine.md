# Creatinine and eGFR — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/creatinine-test/

Serum creatinine reflects how well the kidneys are filtering waste. It is also influenced by muscle mass, age, sex, and recent meat intake. Estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) corrects for those factors.

## Typical adult ranges

- Men: 0.7–1.3 mg/dL.
- Women: 0.6–1.1 mg/dL.

Lower values are often non-pathologic in people with low muscle mass. Higher values can reflect reduced kidney filtration, dehydration, recent intense protein intake, or certain medications.

## eGFR

eGFR is calculated from creatinine plus age and sex; the CKD-EPI 2021 equation removed race as a coefficient. KDIGO categorizes eGFR (mL/min/1.73m²) as:

- 90 or above (G1) — normal or high.
- 60–89 (G2) — mildly decreased.
- 45–59 (G3a) — moderately decreased.
- 30–44 (G3b) — moderately decreased.
- 15–29 (G4) — severely decreased.
- Below 15 (G5) — kidney failure range.

A single low eGFR is not chronic kidney disease. CKD requires sustained reduction (≥3 months) or markers of damage such as albuminuria. Acute drops in eGFR with vomiting, diuretic use, or NSAID exposure are often reversible with hydration and medication review.

## Why creatinine and eGFR can mislead

- Very high or very low muscle mass changes interpretation; consider cystatin C in those situations.
- Pregnancy normally lowers creatinine.
- Certain drugs (trimethoprim, cimetidine, fenofibrate) raise creatinine without changing true filtration.
- Cooked-meat meals can transiently raise creatinine.

## Practical follow-up

A new abnormality is usually repeated to confirm it is real and persistent, then evaluated with urine albumin/creatinine ratio and a basic workup (blood pressure, glucose, medication review). Persistent eGFR under 60, or albuminuria, warrants a clinician visit.
