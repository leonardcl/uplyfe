# Thyroid panel and TSH — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/tsh-thyroid-stimulating-hormone-test/

TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone) is the most sensitive screening test for thyroid problems. The pituitary releases more TSH when the body needs more thyroid hormone and less when it has enough. TSH and free T4 (and sometimes free T3) are interpreted together.

## Typical adult range and patterns

- TSH typical range: 0.4–4.0 mIU/L (some labs use 0.5–5.0).
- High TSH with low free T4 — pattern consistent with overt hypothyroidism.
- High TSH with normal free T4 — pattern consistent with subclinical hypothyroidism.
- Low TSH with high free T4 or T3 — pattern consistent with overt hyperthyroidism.
- Low TSH with normal free T4 and T3 — pattern consistent with subclinical hyperthyroidism.

A TSH of 10 mIU/L or higher with normal free T4 is more often associated with progression to overt hypothyroidism than a milder elevation, and is often treated.

## Free T4 / Free T3

- Free T4 typical range: 0.8–1.8 ng/dL.
- Free T3 typical range: 2.3–4.2 pg/mL.

## Special situations

- Pregnancy uses trimester-specific TSH ranges; non-pregnant cutoffs do not apply.
- Acute illness can transiently lower thyroid hormone levels ("sick euthyroid").
- Biotin (vitamin B7) supplements can interfere with the immunoassay; many clinicians ask people to stop biotin for 2–3 days before testing.
- Some medications (amiodarone, lithium, glucocorticoids, certain seizure drugs) alter results.

## Practical follow-up

A single abnormal TSH in an otherwise well person is usually repeated, and free T4 (and sometimes T3, anti-TPO antibodies) is added before any treatment decision.
