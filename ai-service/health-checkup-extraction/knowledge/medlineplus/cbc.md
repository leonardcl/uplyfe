# Complete blood count (CBC) — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/complete-blood-count-cbc/

A CBC measures red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. It screens for anemia, infection, bleeding/clotting disorders, and bone marrow problems.

## Hemoglobin and hematocrit

Typical adult ranges:

- Men: hemoglobin 13.5–17.5 g/dL; hematocrit 38.8–50.0%.
- Women: hemoglobin 12.0–15.5 g/dL; hematocrit 34.9–44.5%.

Anemia is hemoglobin below the sex-specific lower limit. Severe anemia (hemoglobin under 8 g/dL) warrants prompt evaluation, especially with marked fatigue, shortness of breath, dizziness, or chest pain.

## Mean corpuscular volume (MCV)

The size of red blood cells points toward the cause of anemia:

- MCV under 80 fL — microcytic; iron deficiency and thalassemia trait are the classical causes.
- MCV 80–100 fL — normocytic.
- MCV over 100 fL — macrocytic; B12 / folate deficiency, alcohol use, hypothyroidism, and some medications are common causes.

## White blood cells

Typical adult range: 4.5–11.0 ×10³/µL. Mild elevations are common with infection, inflammation, stress, exercise, smoking, and corticosteroid use. Marked elevation (>30) or marked depression (<2) warrants prompt evaluation.

## Platelets

Typical adult range: 150–450 ×10³/µL. Severe thrombocytopenia (<50) carries bleeding risk; severe thrombocytosis (>1000) carries thrombosis risk. Both warrant evaluation. Mild abnormalities are common and may simply reflect a transient reaction to infection or inflammation.

## Practical context

Reference ranges and the meaning of borderline values vary by laboratory, age, and recent illness. Repeat testing distinguishes a true ongoing abnormality from a transient one. A new significant abnormality should be discussed with a clinician.
