# C-reactive protein (CRP) and ESR — MedlinePlus

Sources:
- https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/c-reactive-protein-crp-test/
- https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/erythrocyte-sedimentation-rate-esr/

CRP and ESR are non-specific inflammation markers. They rise in many conditions and are interpreted together with the clinical picture, not in isolation.

## hs-CRP (high-sensitivity CRP)

The high-sensitivity assay is sensitive enough to use for cardiovascular-risk stratification. AHA/CDC bands:

- Below 1.0 mg/L — lower-risk band.
- 1.0–3.0 mg/L — average-risk band.
- Above 3.0 mg/L — higher-risk band.

Values above 10 mg/L typically reflect acute infection or another non-cardiac inflammatory process and are not specific for cardiovascular risk in that range.

## ESR

Typical adult upper limits (mm/hr):

- Men under 50: about 15.
- Men 50 and older: about 20.
- Women under 50: about 20.
- Women 50 and older: about 30.

ESR can rise with infection, autoimmune disease, malignancy, anemia, pregnancy, and obesity. Marked elevation (over 100 mm/hr) has a narrower differential and warrants clinical evaluation.

## Practical context

Both CRP and ESR can be transiently elevated for days to weeks after infections, surgery, or trauma. A single elevated value should be interpreted alongside symptoms, history, and physical exam, not on its own.
