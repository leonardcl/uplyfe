# Electrolyte panel — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/electrolyte-panel/

An electrolyte panel measures sodium, potassium, chloride, and bicarbonate. It is part of a basic or comprehensive metabolic panel and screens for fluid balance and acid-base problems.

## Sodium (135–145 mmol/L)

Hyponatremia (low sodium) has many causes: medications (especially thiazides and SSRIs), heart failure, kidney disease, hormonal causes (adrenal, thyroid), and excess water intake. Severe hyponatremia (under 120 mmol/L) can cause confusion or seizures and warrants prompt evaluation. Hypernatremia (above 145 mmol/L) usually reflects relative water deficit; severe hypernatremia (over 160) warrants prompt evaluation.

## Potassium (3.5–5.0 mmol/L)

Both hypokalemia (low) and hyperkalemia (high) can affect heart rhythm. Severe values (<2.5 or >6.5 mmol/L) warrant prompt evaluation. Common contributors include diuretics, ACE inhibitors / ARBs, kidney disease, gastrointestinal losses, and dietary intake. A high potassium reading on a hemolyzed sample is a common false positive — repeat testing may be needed.

## Chloride (96–106 mmol/L)

Chloride generally tracks sodium and acid-base status. Isolated chloride changes are rarely clinically directive on their own.

## Calcium

Often included on the same panel. Total calcium typical adult range is 8.5–10.5 mg/dL. Total calcium is influenced by albumin; "corrected calcium" or ionized calcium is used when albumin is abnormal. Severe derangements (<7 or >13 mg/dL) can affect cardiac and neuromuscular function and warrant prompt evaluation.

## Practical notes

Mild deviations are common and may simply reflect hydration, medications, or recent meals. Persistent or symptomatic abnormalities warrant a clinician visit and a medication review.
