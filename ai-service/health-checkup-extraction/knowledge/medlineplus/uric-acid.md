# Uric acid — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/uric-acid-test-blood-analysis/

Uric acid is a breakdown product of purines from food and from normal cell turnover. Most people clear it through the kidneys.

## Typical ranges

- Men: about 3.4–7.0 mg/dL.
- Women: about 2.4–6.0 mg/dL.

Hyperuricemia (men >7, women >6) is associated with gout — when uric acid crystals deposit in joints — and with kidney stones. Many people with elevated uric acid never develop symptoms; asymptomatic hyperuricemia does not always require treatment.

## Common contributors

- High intake of red meat, organ meats, beer, and high-fructose beverages.
- Diuretic use, low-dose aspirin, niacin, cyclosporine.
- Reduced kidney clearance.
- Genetic predisposition.
- Rapid cell turnover (e.g., cancer treatment).

## Symptoms suggesting clinical gout

Sudden, severe joint pain — most often in the big toe, but also ankle, knee, or other joints — with redness, warmth, and swelling. Diagnosis is confirmed by joint fluid examination, not by uric acid alone; uric acid can be normal during an acute attack.

## What to do with an isolated high value

A single elevated uric acid in someone without symptoms is usually managed with lifestyle: hydration, weight management if applicable, reducing red meat / organ meats / beer / sugar-sweetened beverages, and reviewing contributing medications with a clinician.
