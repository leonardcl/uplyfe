# Fasting blood glucose — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/blood-glucose-test/

A fasting blood glucose test measures the amount of sugar (glucose) in the blood after the person has not eaten for at least 8 hours. It is used to screen for diabetes and prediabetes and to monitor people who already have a diagnosis.

## Reference categories

For non-pregnant adults, the test is interpreted using American Diabetes Association cutoffs:

- Normal: less than 100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L).
- Prediabetes (impaired fasting glucose): 100–125 mg/dL (5.6–6.9 mmol/L).
- Diabetes range: 126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L) or higher on more than one occasion.

A single elevated value is not a diagnosis. Confirmation typically requires a second test on a different day, or correlation with HbA1c or an oral glucose tolerance test.

## Reasons a result can be misleading

- The person did not actually fast for 8 hours.
- Recent illness, infection, or steroid use.
- Severe stress or surgery in the prior 24 hours.
- Certain medications, including corticosteroids, some diuretics, and some antipsychotics.
- Pregnancy uses different thresholds (gestational diabetes screening).

## Hypoglycemia

A fasting glucose under 70 mg/dL is hypoglycemia; under 54 mg/dL is severe hypoglycemia and warrants prompt evaluation, especially with symptoms such as confusion, sweating, weakness, or loss of consciousness.

## When to seek prompt care

A fasting glucose at or above 400 mg/dL, or any glucose value with symptoms of diabetic ketoacidosis (vomiting, severe thirst, deep rapid breathing, fruity-smelling breath, confusion), is a medical emergency.

## What's next after an abnormal result

Repeat testing, lifestyle review (diet, weight, activity), and a clinician visit. A normal fasting glucose does not by itself rule out diabetes; an HbA1c or 2-hour postprandial test can add information.
