# Liver function tests (LFTs) — MedlinePlus

Source: MedlinePlus, U.S. National Library of Medicine. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/liver-function-tests/

A liver panel typically includes ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, total and direct bilirubin, and albumin. It screens for liver injury and helps localize a problem to the liver, the biliary tree, or systemic causes.

## What each value tells you

- ALT (alanine aminotransferase, also SGPT) is the most liver-specific aminotransferase. Mild elevations are common with fatty liver, alcohol use, recent intense exercise, viral illness, certain medications, or supplements.
- AST (aspartate aminotransferase, also SGOT) is found in liver, heart, muscle, and red cells. AST elevations without ALT elevations may reflect non-liver causes such as muscle injury or hemolysis.
- ALP (alkaline phosphatase) rises in cholestatic liver disease and in bone-related conditions; GGT helps localize ALP elevations to the liver. Mild GGT elevation is common with alcohol use and many medications.
- Bilirubin: total bilirubin above ~1.2 mg/dL is mildly elevated; visible jaundice typically appears around 2.5–3 mg/dL. Direct (conjugated) bilirubin elevation suggests biliary or hepatocellular cholestasis; indirect (unconjugated) elevation suggests increased red-cell turnover or Gilbert syndrome.
- Albumin: low albumin can reflect liver synthetic dysfunction, malnutrition, or protein loss through gut or kidney; acute illness lowers it temporarily.

## AST/ALT ratio

When both aminotransferases are elevated, an AST/ALT ratio of 2 or higher is classically associated with alcohol-related liver disease, but is not specific. A ratio less than 1 is common in non-alcoholic fatty liver disease and viral hepatitis. The ratio is most informative when the absolute values are clearly elevated.

## When to escalate

- ALT or AST at or above ~10× the upper reference limit (typically ≥400 U/L).
- Total bilirubin above 5 mg/dL, especially with abdominal pain, dark urine, or jaundice.
- New jaundice with confusion or bleeding.
- Sudden severe right-upper-quadrant pain with abnormal LFTs.

## Practical follow-up

Mild, persistent elevations are usually evaluated by reviewing alcohol use, body weight and metabolic risk, medications and supplements, and screening for chronic viral hepatitis and other causes. Results that don't normalize over a few weeks should be discussed with a clinician.
