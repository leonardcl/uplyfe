# When to seek prompt or emergency care

Sources:
- MedlinePlus. https://medlineplus.gov/
- CDC Stroke Signs and Symptoms (FAST). https://www.cdc.gov/stroke/signs-symptoms.html
- ACC/AHA 2017 Hypertension Guideline (hypertensive crisis criteria).

US government content is public domain.

## Call emergency services or go to the ED for any of the following

### Cardiovascular emergencies

- Chest pain or pressure, especially with radiation to the arm, jaw, or back, sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath.
- Sudden severe shortness of breath or shortness of breath with leg swelling.
- Fainting or near-fainting (especially with chest pain or palpitations).
- Heart racing with lightheadedness, fainting, or chest pain.
- Blood pressure ≥180/120 mmHg with any of: chest pain, shortness of breath, vision changes, severe headache, weakness, or confusion (hypertensive crisis).

### Stroke — use FAST

- F — Face drooping on one side.
- A — Arm weakness on one side.
- S — Speech slurred or strange.
- T — Time to call emergency services. Note the time symptoms started; treatments are time-sensitive.

Other stroke signs: sudden severe headache, sudden vision loss, sudden severe dizziness or trouble walking.

### Severe metabolic / endocrine

- Glucose ≥400 mg/dL, especially with vomiting, severe thirst, deep rapid breathing, fruity-smelling breath, or confusion (possible diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar state).
- Repeated low blood sugar with confusion, sweating, or loss of consciousness.

### Severe abdominal / hepatic

- Severe abdominal pain, especially with jaundice, vomiting blood, black tarry stools, or fainting.
- New jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes), especially with confusion, fever, or severe pain.

### Severe hematologic

- Severe bruising or spontaneous bleeding (gums, nose, urine, stool).
- Severe anemia (very low hemoglobin) with marked fatigue, shortness of breath, chest pain, or fainting.
- Marked unintentional weight loss.

### Severe electrolyte derangement (when known from labs)

- Sodium <120 or >160 mmol/L.
- Potassium <2.5 or >6.5 mmol/L.
- Calcium <7 or >13 mg/dL.

These are typically symptomatic (confusion, seizures, weakness, palpitations) and warrant prompt evaluation.

### Severe liver-injury labs

- ALT or AST ≥10× upper limit, especially with new jaundice, abdominal pain, or confusion.
- Total bilirubin >5 mg/dL with abdominal pain.

## Schedule a prompt clinician visit (not an emergency, but soon)

- Any new or markedly abnormal lab finding.
- Persistent abnormalities on repeat testing.
- Findings that affect treatment of an existing condition.
- New or worsening symptoms that don't fit "just feeling tired".

## What is *not* diagnostic

- A single lab value is rarely sufficient to diagnose any condition.
- Reference ranges vary by laboratory.
- Many results are influenced by medications, diet, hydration, recent illness, exercise, and timing of the blood draw.
- Normal labs do not always rule out disease; symptoms still matter and may need additional testing.
