# ADA Standards of Care 2024 — glucose screening summary

Source: American Diabetes Association. Standards of Care in Diabetes — 2024. Diabetes Care. https://diabetesjournals.org/care/issue/47/Supplement_1
This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval, not a verbatim reproduction.

## Diagnostic criteria for diabetes (any one, confirmed)

- Fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L). Fasting means no caloric intake for at least 8 hours.
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) during a 75-g oral glucose tolerance test.
- HbA1c ≥6.5% (using an NGSP-certified, DCCT-aligned method).
- In a person with classic hyperglycemia symptoms or hyperglycemic crisis, a random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL.

When the result is unequivocally elevated, a single test can be diagnostic; otherwise, a confirmatory second test is recommended on a separate day.

## Prediabetes

Increased risk for diabetes is identified by any one of:

- Impaired fasting glucose: fasting plasma glucose 100–125 mg/dL (5.6–6.9 mmol/L).
- Impaired glucose tolerance: 2-hour OGTT 140–199 mg/dL (7.8–11.0 mmol/L).
- HbA1c 5.7%–6.4%.

Risk rises continuously across these ranges; the higher the value, the greater the absolute risk.

## Who and when to screen

Routine screening is recommended for asymptomatic adults with:

- BMI ≥25 kg/m² (or ≥23 kg/m² for those of Asian descent) plus one or more risk factors (family history, hypertension, dyslipidemia, physical inactivity, polycystic ovary syndrome, history of gestational diabetes, or vascular disease).
- All adults from age 35 (lower in some recent updates), repeated at least every 3 years if normal, more often if results trend toward prediabetes.

## Lifestyle therapy first

For prediabetes, structured lifestyle programs (modeled on the Diabetes Prevention Program) targeting ~7% weight loss and ≥150 minutes/week of moderate activity reduce progression to type 2 diabetes by roughly half. The DPP is the strongest evidence base behind ADA's lifestyle-first recommendation for prediabetes.

## When to seek prompt care

Severe hyperglycemia (very high glucose with symptoms such as severe thirst, frequent urination, vomiting, deep rapid breathing, fruity breath, confusion) is a medical emergency and warrants prompt evaluation regardless of the prior diagnosis.
