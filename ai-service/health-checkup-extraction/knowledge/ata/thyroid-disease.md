# American Thyroid Association — interpreting thyroid tests

Source: American Thyroid Association practice guidelines. https://www.thyroid.org/professionals/ata-professional-guidelines/
This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval, not a verbatim reproduction.

## TSH first

TSH is the most sensitive test. Normal TSH with no symptoms generally rules out clinically important primary thyroid disease. When TSH is abnormal, free T4 (and sometimes free T3) is added.

## Patterns

- High TSH + low free T4 — overt hypothyroidism. Most often autoimmune (Hashimoto's). Symptoms include fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, dry skin, constipation.
- High TSH + normal free T4 — subclinical hypothyroidism. Treatment is usually considered when TSH is ≥10 mIU/L, or when symptoms or anti-TPO antibodies are present, or in pregnancy / pregnancy planning.
- Low TSH + high free T4/T3 — overt hyperthyroidism. Common causes: Graves' disease, toxic nodule, thyroiditis. Symptoms include palpitations, tremor, weight loss, heat intolerance, anxiety.
- Low TSH + normal free T4/T3 — subclinical hyperthyroidism. Treatment varies with age, TSH severity, and atrial fibrillation / osteoporosis risk.

## Pregnancy

Pregnancy changes thyroid physiology and *non-pregnant TSH cutoffs do not apply*. ATA pregnancy-specific guidance suggests trimester-specific TSH ranges (often loosely 0.1–4.0 mIU/L in the first trimester with adjustments later); local laboratory ranges should be used when available. Untreated overt hypothyroidism in pregnancy is associated with adverse outcomes; subclinical hypothyroidism management depends on TSH level and TPO-antibody status.

## Pitfalls

- Acute non-thyroidal illness can transiently lower TSH and free T3 ("euthyroid sick syndrome"). Wait until recovery to interpret unless severely abnormal.
- Biotin (vitamin B7) supplements interfere with many thyroid immunoassays; commonly recommended to stop biotin 2–3 days before testing.
- Drugs that affect results: amiodarone, lithium, glucocorticoids, dopamine, certain seizure medications, immune-checkpoint inhibitors.

## Practical follow-up

A single isolated TSH abnormality in an otherwise well person is usually repeated, with addition of free T4 (and TPO antibodies in suspected Hashimoto's) before any treatment decision. Most thyroid problems are slow and non-urgent; repeat testing across weeks-to-months is the norm.
