# Dietary Guidelines for Americans, 2020–2025

Source: U.S. Department of Agriculture and U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. Dietary Guidelines for Americans, 2020-2025. 9th Edition. December 2020. https://www.dietaryguidelines.gov/
US government work; public domain. This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval.

## Four core guidelines

1. Follow a healthy dietary pattern at every life stage.
2. Customize and enjoy nutrient-dense food and beverage choices to reflect personal preferences, cultural traditions, and budgetary considerations.
3. Focus on meeting food group needs with nutrient-dense foods and beverages, and stay within calorie limits.
4. Limit foods and beverages higher in added sugars, saturated fat, and sodium, and limit alcoholic beverages.

## What "nutrient-dense" looks like

A healthy dietary pattern emphasizes:

- Vegetables of all types — dark green; red and orange; beans, peas, lentils; starchy; other.
- Fruits, especially whole fruit.
- Grains, with at least half being whole grains.
- Dairy, including fat-free or low-fat milk, yogurt, and cheese, and/or lactose-free versions and fortified soy alternatives.
- Protein foods, including lean meats, poultry, eggs, seafood, beans/peas/lentils, nuts, seeds, and soy products.
- Oils, including vegetable oils and oils in food, such as seafood and nuts.

## Key quantitative limits

- Added sugars: <10% of calories per day starting at age 2; below 10% in pregnancy and lactation; avoid added sugars before age 2.
- Saturated fat: <10% of calories per day starting at age 2.
- Sodium: <2300 mg/day for ages 14+ and lower for younger ages; lower targets are appropriate for many adults with hypertension.
- Alcohol: adults of legal age who choose to drink should limit intake — for men, 2 drinks or less per day; for women, 1 drink or less per day; and many should drink less or not at all.

## Why patterns, not single foods

The guidelines emphasize *patterns* of eating because most evidence on diet and chronic disease is strongest at the dietary-pattern level (e.g., Mediterranean-style, DASH, Healthy U.S.-Style). No single food is required, and the same pattern can be built within many cultural and budgetary contexts.

## Cardiometabolic translations

For people with elevated lipids, blood pressure, or glucose, the broad DGA pattern overlaps strongly with:

- AHA: limit saturated fat to <6% of calories; emphasize unsaturated fats from plants and seafood.
- ADA nutrition therapy: emphasize non-starchy vegetables, minimize added sugars and refined grains, prefer water as the beverage of choice.
- DASH: 4–5 servings/day each of vegetables and fruits; whole grains; nuts/seeds/legumes 4–5 times/week; fat-free or low-fat dairy; lean protein.

## Practical context

The guidelines are population-level. Individuals with kidney disease, food allergies, pregnancy, lactation, eating disorders, or specific clinical conditions should adapt these defaults with a clinician or registered dietitian.
