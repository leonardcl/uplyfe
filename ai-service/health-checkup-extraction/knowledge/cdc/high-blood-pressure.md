# High blood pressure — CDC overview

Source: CDC. About High Blood Pressure. https://www.cdc.gov/high-blood-pressure/about/
CDC content is public domain. This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval.

## Why it matters

High blood pressure (hypertension) is the leading modifiable risk factor for stroke, heart attack, heart failure, and kidney disease. It often has no symptoms; it is detected through measurement, not feel.

## Categories (mmHg)

ACC/AHA 2017 categories:

- Normal: <120 / <80.
- Elevated: 120–129 / <80.
- Stage 1 hypertension: 130–139 / 80–89.
- Stage 2 hypertension: ≥140 / ≥90.
- Hypertensive crisis: ≥180 and/or ≥120 — prompt evaluation.

A single reading is not a diagnosis. Confirm with multiple readings on different days, or with home / ambulatory monitoring, before starting therapy.

## Lifestyle changes that lower blood pressure

- Lose weight if overweight (every kg of weight loss can reduce systolic BP by ~1 mmHg in many people).
- Follow a DASH-style eating pattern (rich in fruit, vegetables, low-fat dairy, whole grains).
- Reduce sodium (toward 1500–2300 mg/day).
- Increase potassium intake from food (unless kidney disease or potassium-elevating medications make this inadvisable).
- Aerobic exercise 90–150 minutes per week.
- Limit alcohol (≤2 drinks/day men, ≤1/day women).
- Stop smoking.
- Manage stress and prioritize sleep (untreated obstructive sleep apnea raises BP substantially).

## Measurement quality

Many "high" readings are not real hypertension; they reflect technique. For reliable readings:

- Sit quietly for 5 minutes before measurement.
- Empty bladder.
- Feet flat on floor, back supported, arm at heart level.
- No caffeine, exercise, or smoking 30 minutes before.
- Take 2 readings, 1 minute apart, and average them.

## When to call for help

A blood pressure of 180/120 or higher with chest pain, shortness of breath, vision changes, severe headache, weakness, or new neurologic symptoms is a medical emergency. Even without symptoms, very high readings should be re-checked promptly and discussed with a clinician.
