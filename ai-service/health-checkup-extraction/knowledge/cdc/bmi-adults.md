# BMI for adults — CDC

Source: CDC. About Adult BMI. https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-weight-growth/bmi/about-adult-bmi.html
This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval. CDC content is public domain.

## What BMI is

Body Mass Index (BMI) = weight (kg) / height (m)². It's a screening tool, not a diagnostic measure of body composition. It does not directly measure body fat.

## Adult categories (kg/m²)

- Below 18.5 — underweight.
- 18.5–24.9 — healthy weight.
- 25.0–29.9 — overweight.
- 30.0–34.9 — class I obesity.
- 35.0–39.9 — class II obesity.
- 40 or above — class III obesity.

## Where BMI can mislead

- Very muscular adults can have a high BMI without excess fat.
- Older adults with reduced muscle mass can have a "normal" BMI while having metabolically problematic body composition.
- Pregnant and lactating people — BMI is not the right tool.
- BMI does not capture fat distribution; central adiposity (waist circumference) carries metabolic risk independent of BMI.

## Population-specific thresholds

The WHO Asian-population guidance recommends lower thresholds (overweight ≥23, obesity ≥27.5 kg/m²) because cardiometabolic risk rises at lower BMIs in some Asian populations. Use the cutoffs that match the patient's population when interpreting screening results.

## Companion measures

- Waist circumference: men ≥102 cm (40 in), women ≥88 cm (35 in) signals elevated central adiposity (NCEP ATP III). Lower cutoffs (~90 cm men, ~80 cm women) are recommended for many Asian populations.
- Waist-to-hip ratio and waist-to-height ratio (>0.5) are alternatives that some practitioners prefer.

## Practical context

A single BMI in the overweight or obesity range is not a diagnosis on its own. Cardiometabolic risk should be evaluated alongside blood pressure, lipids, glucose, family history, and lifestyle. Modest, sustainable weight loss (5–10%) meaningfully improves blood pressure, glucose, and lipids in adults with overweight or obesity.
