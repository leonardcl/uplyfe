# Physical activity for adults — CDC

Source: CDC. Adult Activity: An Overview. https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html
CDC content is public domain. This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval.

## Weekly target

For substantial health benefits, adults should do EITHER:

- ≥150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, OR
- ≥75 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity, OR
- An equivalent combination of moderate and vigorous activity.

PLUS

- Muscle-strengthening activities involving all major muscle groups on ≥2 days/week.

For greater health benefits, doubling the aerobic target (≥300 min/week moderate or ≥150 min/week vigorous) is associated with additional cardiovascular and metabolic benefit.

## Intensity test

- Moderate intensity: heart rate up, breathing harder, can talk but not sing.
- Vigorous intensity: heart rate substantially elevated, can say only a few words before pausing for breath.

Examples of moderate: brisk walking (~3 mph or faster), water aerobics, riding a bike on flat ground, doubles tennis.
Examples of vigorous: jogging or running, fast cycling, singles tennis, swimming laps, jumping rope.

## Strength training

Train all major muscle groups (legs, hips, back, abdomen, chest, shoulders, arms) at moderate or greater effort on ≥2 days/week. Examples: weight training, body-weight exercises (push-ups, squats), heavy gardening, resistance bands.

## How to start if you're new to activity

- Start with 10–15 minute bouts and build up.
- Any activity is better than none — even short sessions count.
- Spread activity across most days of the week rather than concentrating on one day.

## Older adults and balance

Older adults add multicomponent activities that include balance training (e.g., tai chi, single-leg standing) on ≥3 days/week. Falls prevention is a meaningful goal.

## Cardiometabolic-specific notes

- For prediabetes / type 2 diabetes: combining aerobic and resistance training has stronger glycemic effects than either alone. A short walk after meals can blunt post-meal glucose rises.
- For hypertension: regular aerobic exercise lowers systolic BP by ~5 mmHg on average; isometric exercises (planks, wall sits) also have evidence.
- For weight management: 200–300 minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity is more effective than the 150-minute minimum.

## Safety

People with cardiovascular disease, severe orthopedic limitations, uncontrolled hypertension, or significant lab abnormalities should discuss exercise plans with a clinician before starting a vigorous program. Stop and seek care for chest pain, severe shortness of breath, syncope, or new neurologic symptoms during exercise.
