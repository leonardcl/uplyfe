# AASLD/ACG 2017 — Evaluation of abnormal liver chemistries

Source: ACG Clinical Guideline: Evaluation of Abnormal Liver Chemistries. Am J Gastroenterol. 2017.
Available at: https://journals.lww.com/ajg/Fulltext/2017/01000/ACG_Clinical_Guideline__Evaluation_of_Abnormal.10.aspx
This is a paraphrased summary for retrieval, not a verbatim reproduction.

## Defining "abnormal"

The guideline emphasizes that historic ALT upper limits (often ~40 U/L) are higher than the true population-distribution-derived upper limits. They suggest a "true healthy" ALT upper limit closer to 29–33 U/L for men and 19–25 U/L for women, but acknowledge that most labs still report ~40 U/L.

For screening interpretation, classifying ALT or AST elevations is useful:

- Borderline: up to 2× the upper reference limit.
- Mild-moderate: 2–5× upper limit.
- Severe: >5× upper limit; values ≥10× upper limit warrant urgent evaluation for acute liver injury.

## Common causes of mild persistent elevations

Most common contributors to mildly elevated aminotransferases in primary care:

- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (most common in adults with metabolic risk).
- Alcohol-related liver disease.
- Medication- or supplement-induced (statins, antibiotics, antiepileptics, herbals, anabolic steroids).
- Chronic viral hepatitis (HBV, HCV).
- Recent strenuous exercise (transient).
- Hemolysis or muscle injury (AST out of proportion to ALT).
- Less commonly: autoimmune hepatitis, hemochromatosis, alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency, Wilson disease, celiac disease, thyroid disease.

## Suggested initial workup for persistent mild elevation

- Confirm by repeat testing.
- Review alcohol use, medications, and supplements.
- Calculate BMI and check metabolic risk markers (lipids, glucose, HbA1c).
- HBV / HCV serology.
- Iron studies, ANA + smooth muscle antibody, ceruloplasmin (if young), alpha-1-antitrypsin, TSH, tissue transglutaminase if symptoms.
- Liver imaging (ultrasound) for fatty liver and structural disease.

## When to escalate

Acutely severe elevations (≥10× upper limit), new jaundice, signs of synthetic dysfunction (low albumin, prolonged INR), or symptoms (RUQ pain, fever, confusion) warrant prompt clinical evaluation.

## Practical context

A single mild elevation in an asymptomatic person is rarely an emergency, but it should not be ignored — persistent abnormalities benefit from a structured workup.
