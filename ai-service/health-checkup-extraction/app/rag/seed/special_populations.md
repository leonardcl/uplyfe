# Special populations — pediatric, pregnancy, older adults

Source: AAP Bright Futures Guidelines; ACOG Committee Opinions on prenatal lab values; American Geriatrics Society reference standards.

Reference ranges in this report assume a non-pregnant adult. The notes below flag the most important deviations.

## Pediatric (under 18 years)

Most adult thresholds DO NOT apply directly to children. Examples:
- Hemoglobin: lower in children (11 to 14 g/dL is normal for many ages).
- Cholesterol targets are not the same — pediatric LDL screening starts at age 9 to 11 with universal screening, and thresholds are age-specific.
- Liver enzymes (ALT, AST): infants and young children often have higher reference ranges than adults.
- Creatinine and eGFR: children's normal ranges are lower than adults; adult eGFR formulas (CKD-EPI) are not validated for ages under 18 — pediatric labs use Schwartz or CKiD U25 instead.
- HbA1c diagnostic cutoffs are the same as adults (5.7 to 6.4 percent prediabetes; 6.5 percent or higher diabetes), but lifestyle programs are adapted for the family rather than the child alone.

If you are interpreting a child's lab report, the lab's printed reference range is more reliable than any global threshold — and a pediatric clinician should confirm any flagged result.

## Pregnancy

Pregnancy shifts many normal ranges, sometimes substantially:
- Hemoglobin and hematocrit fall in normal pregnancy from hemodilution; the lower threshold for anemia is 11 g/dL in the first and third trimesters and 10.5 g/dL in the second.
- Total cholesterol and triglycerides rise during pregnancy and aren't treated the same way as in non-pregnant adults.
- Glucose targets are tighter in pregnancy. Fasting greater than 92 mg/dL or 1-hour OGTT greater than 180 mg/dL or 2-hour greater than 153 mg/dL meets criteria for gestational diabetes (IADPSG / ACOG).
- Thyroid: TSH targets are trimester-specific; the 0.27 to 4.2 mIU/L general adult range is not the goal in pregnancy.
- Blood pressure: 140/90 mmHg or higher after 20 weeks suggests gestational hypertension; with proteinuria or end-organ symptoms it may be preeclampsia and warrants prompt evaluation.

This report is not validated for pregnancy. Always discuss prenatal results with an obstetrician or midwife.

## Older adults (65 and older)

- HbA1c targets may be relaxed (under 7.5 to 8 percent acceptable for older adults with multiple comorbidities) — overly tight glucose control causes more falls and hypoglycemia than benefit in this group.
- Blood pressure targets may also be slightly higher in frail older adults; sudden aggressive lowering can cause falls.
- Creatinine: muscle mass declines with age, so a "normal" creatinine in an older adult can mask reduced kidney function. eGFR is the better marker.
- Vitamin D and B12 deficiency are more common; symptoms (fatigue, neuropathy, confusion) are easy to miss.
- Polypharmacy interactions affect many lab values.

## What this means for the report

This system uses general adult reference ranges by default, then prefers the lab's printed reference range when present. Pediatric or pregnant patients should ALWAYS verify with a clinician — and if the report doesn't print explicit ranges, the system's interpretation may not apply.
