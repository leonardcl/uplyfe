# Liver enzymes — interpretation notes

Source: AASLD ALT guidance; AACC reference intervals.

## ALT and AST

ALT (SGPT) is more specific to liver injury than AST (SGOT). Mild elevations are common and often reflect fatty liver, alcohol use, recent intense exercise, viral illness, or medication effects (including over-the-counter drugs and supplements).

Typical adult upper limits used in screening: ALT ≤40 U/L (some labs use ≤30 men / ≤19 women), AST ≤40 U/L. Values >2× upper limit are reproducibly elevated; ≥10× upper limit is markedly elevated and warrants prompt evaluation.

## AST/ALT ratio (De Ritis)

When both are elevated, an AST/ALT ratio ≥2 is classically associated with alcohol-related liver disease, but is not specific. Ratios <1 are common in fatty liver and viral hepatitis. The ratio is informative only when enzymes are clearly elevated.

## ALP and GGT

ALP can rise in cholestatic patterns and in bone-related conditions; GGT helps localize ALP elevations to the liver. Mild GGT elevation is common with alcohol use and certain medications.

## Bilirubin

Total bilirubin >1.2 mg/dL is mildly elevated. Visible jaundice typically appears around 2.5–3 mg/dL. Direct (conjugated) bilirubin elevation suggests biliary or hepatocellular cholestasis; indirect (unconjugated) elevation suggests increased red-cell turnover or Gilbert syndrome.

## Albumin

Low albumin can reflect liver synthetic dysfunction, malnutrition, or protein loss (kidney/gut). Acute illness lowers albumin temporarily.

## When to escalate

Aminotransferases ≥10× upper limit, total bilirubin >5 mg/dL, or new jaundice with abdominal pain warrant prompt clinical evaluation.
