# Lifestyle factors that affect lab results

Source: U.S. Department of Health & Human Services 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines (2nd ed.); CDC; AHA scientific statements on sleep and cardiovascular health (Circulation 2016, 2022).

These cross-cutting factors influence many biomarkers simultaneously — sleep, stress, alcohol, smoking, and physical activity all show up across glucose, lipids, blood pressure, and inflammation panels.

## Sleep

Adults benefit from 7 or more hours per night. Less than 7 hours is associated with elevated fasting glucose, higher BMI, and higher cardiovascular risk over time.

Practical anchors:
- Keep a consistent wake time, even on weekends.
- Cut caffeine 8 hours before bed.
- Reserve bed for sleep; phones and TVs delay sleep onset.

## Stress

Chronic stress elevates cortisol, blood pressure, fasting glucose, and inflammation markers (CRP, ESR). Brief stress is normal — sustained months-long stress is the issue.

What helps according to the evidence base:
- 10–20 minutes daily of paced breathing, mindfulness, or progressive muscle relaxation.
- Regular physical activity (any kind).
- Social connection — isolation worsens cardiovascular outcomes independent of other factors.

## Alcohol

Alcohol raises triglycerides, weight, and blood pressure. It can also worsen liver enzymes (especially GGT, ALT) and elevate uric acid.

The 2020-2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans recommend that adults of legal drinking age either choose not to drink or drink in moderation — defined as up to 1 drink/day for women and up to 2 drinks/day for men. Less is healthier; not drinking is also a valid choice.

## Smoking and tobacco

Smoking damages the cardiovascular system, lowers HDL, raises blood pressure, and is the single largest preventable cause of disease across most lab profiles. Quitting at any age improves outcomes; benefits begin within weeks.

## Physical activity

The 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines (2nd edition) recommend for adults:
- 150 to 300 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, OR 75 to 150 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity, OR an equivalent combination.
- Muscle-strengthening activity on 2 or more days per week, working all major muscle groups.
- Some activity is better than none — the largest health gains come when previously inactive people start moving.

For older adults: add balance training (e.g., tai chi, single-leg standing) at least 3 days per week to reduce fall risk.

## Why these matter for the report

When you see borderline-high glucose, blood pressure, triglycerides, or inflammation markers together, lifestyle changes — sleep, stress, alcohol, activity — typically deliver more improvement than any single supplement or food choice.
