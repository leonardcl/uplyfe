# Diet guidance — common evidence-grounded advice

Sources: 2020–2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans; American Heart Association; American Diabetes Association.

## For elevated LDL or total cholesterol

- Limit saturated fat to less than ~6% of total calories (AHA).
- Replace saturated fats (fatty cuts of meat, butter, full-fat dairy) with unsaturated fats (olive oil, canola oil, nuts, seeds, fatty fish).
- Increase soluble fiber (oats, beans, lentils, fruit) — 5–10 g/day of soluble fiber meaningfully lowers LDL.
- Reduce trans fats; check labels for partially hydrogenated oils.
- Plant sterols (~2 g/day) can additionally lower LDL.

## For elevated triglycerides

- Reduce added sugars and refined starches (sugary drinks, sweets, white bread).
- Reduce alcohol — even moderate intake can substantially raise triglycerides in susceptible individuals.
- Increase omega-3 intake (fatty fish 2× per week, or as advised).
- Reach and maintain a healthy weight; even 5–10% weight loss often lowers triglycerides.

## For elevated glucose / HbA1c

- Choose complex carbohydrates (whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruit) over refined carbohydrates.
- Limit sugar-sweetened beverages — these are the single highest-impact target.
- Keep portion sizes moderate; pair carbohydrates with protein, fiber, or healthy fat.
- Mediterranean-style and DASH-style eating patterns have the strongest randomized evidence for cardiometabolic outcomes.

## For elevated blood pressure

- Reduce sodium toward 1500–2300 mg/day (DASH).
- Increase potassium-rich foods (leafy greens, bananas, beans, potatoes, yogurt) unless kidney disease or potassium-elevating medications make this inadvisable.
- Limit alcohol; aim for ≤1 drink/day women, ≤2 drinks/day men if drinking.

## General principles

- Build meals around vegetables, fruit, whole grains, legumes, nuts, fish, and lean protein.
- Limit processed foods, red and processed meats, and ultra-processed snacks and beverages.
- Hydrate primarily with water.
- Personalize: pregnancy, kidney disease, food allergies, and medications can change these defaults — discuss specifics with a clinician or registered dietitian.
