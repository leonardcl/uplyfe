# Vitamin D and B12 — interpretation notes

Source: NIH Office of Dietary Supplements.

## 25-OH Vitamin D

Categories vary by guideline. NIH ODS uses:

- <12 ng/mL — deficiency, risk of bone disease
- 12–19 ng/mL — inadequate for bone and overall health in many adults
- 20–50 ng/mL — generally adequate
- >100 ng/mL — possibly high; very high levels can cause adverse effects

Lower vitamin D is common in people with limited sun exposure, darker skin pigmentation, obesity, malabsorption, and some kidney/liver conditions. Repletion strategies include sunlight exposure, fortified foods, fatty fish, and supplements; specifics should be individualized with a clinician.

## Vitamin B12

NIH ODS bands:

- <200 pg/mL — deficiency range
- 200–300 pg/mL — borderline
- ≥300 pg/mL — generally adequate

B12 deficiency can cause macrocytic anemia and neurologic symptoms (numbness, balance problems, cognitive change). Risk is higher in people with vegan diets, atrophic gastritis, prior gastric/ileal surgery, and long-term proton-pump-inhibitor or metformin use. Methylmalonic acid and homocysteine are sometimes added when B12 is borderline.

## Practical notes

Single low values should be repeated and interpreted alongside the CBC indices (especially MCV) and clinical context.
