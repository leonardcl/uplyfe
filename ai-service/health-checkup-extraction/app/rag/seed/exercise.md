# Exercise guidance — common evidence-grounded advice

Sources: U.S. Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans (2nd ed.); CDC; ACSM.

## General adult target

- ≥150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, OR
- ≥75 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity, OR
- An equivalent combination, spread across most days.
- Plus muscle-strengthening activities involving major muscle groups on ≥2 days/week.

Older adults add balance training (e.g., tai chi, single-leg standing) at least 3 days/week.

## Practical structure

- "Talk test" intensity: moderate = can talk, not sing; vigorous = can say only a few words.
- Break it up: 10–15 minute brisk walks count.
- Progress gradually; people new to exercise often start with 5–10 minutes/day and increase weekly.

## Cardiometabolic-specific considerations

- For glucose / prediabetes: combining aerobic and resistance training has stronger glycemic effects than either alone. A short walk after meals can reduce post-meal glucose excursions.
- For blood pressure: regular aerobic exercise lowers systolic BP by ~5 mmHg on average. Isometric exercises (planks, wall sits) also have evidence.
- For weight management: 200–300 minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity is more effective than the 150-minute minimum.

## Safety

People with cardiovascular disease, severe orthopedic limitations, uncontrolled hypertension, or significant lab abnormalities should discuss exercise plans with a clinician before starting a vigorous program. Stop and seek care for chest pain, severe shortness of breath, syncope, or new neurologic symptoms during exercise.
