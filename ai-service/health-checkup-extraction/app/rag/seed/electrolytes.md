# Electrolytes — interpretation notes

Source: AACC adult electrolyte reference intervals; MedlinePlus.

## Sodium (135–145 mmol/L)

Hyponatremia is common and has many causes (medications, heart failure, kidney disease, hormonal causes, excess water intake). Severe hyponatremia (<120 mmol/L) can cause confusion or seizures and warrants prompt evaluation. Hypernatremia (>145) usually reflects relative water deficit; severe hypernatremia (>160) warrants prompt evaluation.

## Potassium (3.5–5.0 mmol/L)

Both hypokalemia (<3.5) and hyperkalemia (>5.0) can affect heart rhythm. Severe values (<2.5 or >6.5) warrant prompt evaluation. Common contributors: diuretics, ACE inhibitors / ARBs, kidney disease, gastrointestinal losses, and dietary intake. A high potassium reading on a hemolyzed sample can be a false positive — repeat testing may be needed.

## Chloride (96–106 mmol/L)

Chloride generally tracks sodium and acid-base status; isolated chloride changes are rarely clinically directive on their own.

## Calcium (8.5–10.5 mg/dL)

Total calcium is influenced by albumin; "corrected calcium" or ionized calcium is often used when albumin is abnormal. Severe derangements (<7 or >13 mg/dL) can affect cardiac and neuromuscular function and warrant prompt evaluation.

## Practical notes

Ranges vary by lab. Mild deviations are common and may simply reflect hydration, medications, or recent meals. Discuss persistent or symptomatic abnormalities with a clinician.
