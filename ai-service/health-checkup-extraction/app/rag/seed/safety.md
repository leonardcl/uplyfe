# Safety and urgent-care guidance

Source: MedlinePlus; CDC; ACC/AHA hypertension guideline.

## Always discuss results with a clinician for

- Any new or markedly abnormal lab finding
- Persistent abnormalities on repeat testing
- Findings that affect treatment of an existing condition

## Seek prompt medical attention for any of the following

- Chest pain, pressure, tightness, or radiation to the jaw or arm
- Sudden shortness of breath, especially with leg swelling or chest pain
- New severe headache, vision changes, weakness on one side, slurred speech, or facial droop (possible stroke)
- Confusion, fainting, or new unsteadiness
- Severe abdominal pain, especially with jaundice or vomiting blood
- Black or bloody stools
- New jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes)
- Marked unintentional weight loss
- Repeated low blood sugars with confusion, sweating, or loss of consciousness
- Blood pressure ≥180/120 with symptoms (chest pain, shortness of breath, vision changes, or neurologic symptoms) — this is a medical emergency
- Severe anemia (hemoglobin very low) with marked fatigue, shortness of breath, or chest pain
- Glucose ≥400 mg/dL or HbA1c ≥10% — discuss with a clinician promptly
- Severe potassium derangement (<2.5 or >6.5 mmol/L)
- Severe sodium derangement (<120 or >160 mmol/L)
- Severe calcium derangement (<7 or >13 mg/dL)
- Aminotransferases ≥10× upper limit, or new jaundice with abdominal pain

## What is NOT diagnostic

- A single lab value is rarely sufficient to diagnose any condition.
- Reference ranges vary by laboratory.
- Many results are influenced by medications, diet, hydration, recent illness, exercise, and timing of the blood draw.
- Normal labs do not always rule out disease; symptoms still matter and may need additional testing.

## What this report is

Informational only. It supports — it does not replace — a conversation with a qualified clinician.
