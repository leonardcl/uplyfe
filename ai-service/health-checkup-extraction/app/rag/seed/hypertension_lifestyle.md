# Blood pressure — DASH, sodium, potassium, and movement

Source: 2017 ACC/AHA Hypertension Guideline; DASH trial (NEJM 1997, AJCN 2001); 2020-2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans.

The lifestyle anchors below have RCT-grade evidence — they're not "nice-to-haves," they actually change blood pressure.

## DASH eating pattern

The DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) pattern lowered systolic blood pressure by an average of 5 to 6 mmHg in trials, and more in adults with hypertension.

Daily anchors:
- Vegetables: 4 to 5 servings.
- Fruit: 4 to 5 servings.
- Whole grains: 6 to 8 servings.
- Low-fat dairy: 2 to 3 servings.
- Lean protein, fish, poultry: 6 or fewer 1-ounce servings.
- Nuts, seeds, legumes: 4 to 5 servings per week.
- Fats and oils: 2 to 3 servings.
- Sweets: 5 or fewer per week.

## Sodium

Lowering sodium intake to no more than 2,300 mg per day is the general adult target; the DASH-Sodium trial showed an additional 1 to 3 mmHg reduction at 1,500 mg per day, especially in older adults and people with hypertension.

Most sodium in modern diets is hidden in packaged foods, restaurant meals, breads, and processed meats — not the salt shaker.

## Potassium

Potassium offsets sodium's effect. Adults benefit from 3,500 to 4,700 mg per day from food: leafy greens, beans, lentils, sweet potato, banana, yogurt, salmon, avocado.

People with kidney disease should NOT increase potassium without speaking to a clinician — high potassium can be dangerous in that setting.

## Activity

150 to 300 minutes per week of moderate aerobic activity lowers systolic blood pressure by 5 to 8 mmHg in adults with hypertension. Resistance training adds another 2 to 4 mmHg. Both are additive with diet changes.

## Weight

Each 1 kg of sustained weight loss lowers systolic blood pressure by approximately 1 mmHg in adults with overweight, on average.

## Alcohol

Limit alcohol — no more than 2 drinks per day for men, 1 per day for women. Heavy drinking is one of the most common reversible causes of resistant hypertension.

## Stress

Brief daily relaxation practice — paced breathing, meditation, prayer — modestly lowers blood pressure (2 to 5 mmHg) when sustained over months.

## When to escalate

Stage 2 hypertension (140/90 or higher), Stage 1 with cardiovascular risk factors, or any single very high reading (180/120 or higher with symptoms) deserves a clinician visit promptly. Lifestyle alone may not be enough — but it always helps alongside medication when medication is needed.
