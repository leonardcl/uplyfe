# Dyslipidemia — diet and lifestyle anchors

Source: 2018 AHA/ACC/Multisociety Guideline on the Management of Blood Cholesterol; 2021 AHA Dietary Guidance to Improve Cardiovascular Health (Circulation 2021).

Dyslipidemia is a label for any unhealthy mix of cholesterol and triglycerides. The everyday changes below have the strongest evidence — many are useful even when medication is already prescribed.

## Diet anchors

- Replace saturated fat with unsaturated fat. Olive oil, canola oil, nuts, seeds, avocado, and fatty fish (salmon, sardines) lower LDL more reliably than cutting total fat. Limiting saturated fat to less than 6 percent of total calories is the AHA target for high cholesterol.
- Increase soluble fiber. 5–10 g per day of soluble fiber from oats, beans, lentils, apples, citrus, and barley lowers LDL by an average of 5 to 10 mg/dL.
- Choose plant protein when possible. Beans, lentils, tofu, and tempeh in place of red and processed meat reduce LDL and saturated-fat intake.
- Limit added sugars and refined starches. These raise triglycerides quickly. Sweetened beverages are the largest single contributor in many diets.
- Limit alcohol. Even moderate intake raises triglycerides.
- Eat fish at least twice a week. Omega-3 fats (EPA, DHA) lower triglycerides; the strongest effect is at the higher end of weekly fish intake.
- A Mediterranean or DASH eating pattern — vegetables, fruit, whole grains, legumes, nuts, fish, olive oil — outperforms isolated single-food changes in randomized trials (PREDIMED, DASH).

## Activity anchors

- Aerobic activity raises HDL by a small but consistent amount and lowers triglycerides.
- Aim for at least 150 minutes per week moderate or 75 minutes vigorous; more is better up to about 300 minutes per week before benefit plateaus.
- Resistance training improves body composition and insulin sensitivity, both of which support a healthier lipid profile.

## Weight

Even a 5 to 10 percent weight loss in people who are overweight measurably lowers triglycerides and raises HDL. Sustainable changes outperform extreme diets.

## When to discuss medication with a clinician

Discuss with a clinician — do not self-medicate — when LDL is persistently 190 mg/dL or higher, when you have established cardiovascular disease, when you have diabetes plus other risk factors, or when lifestyle changes plus 3 to 6 months of effort haven't moved the numbers enough.
